01 - Problem Statement

Free trial users were redeeming one deal, then leaving - never discovering why they'd signed up.

NeoTaste acquires users primarily through a 1-month free trial. But cohort analysis showed a clear bottleneck: most new users redeemed just 1 deal in their trial period, then uninstalled or disengaged.

This wasn't a product quality problem. The issue was that new users weren't understanding the app's full value quickly enough. They'd discover one deal, use it, and see no reason to return - missing personalisation, saved restaurants, the map view, reviews, and the full range of what NeoTaste offered.

The business problem was trial-to-paid conversion. The user problem was insufficient feature discovery. How do we guide new users through the core features that predict long-term engagement, in their first sessions, so they convert to paid members?

I led the feature design in partnership with the UX Researcher, Data team, and PM. We grounded the entire solution in behavioural data, not assumptions.

02 - Hypothesis

Guided feature discovery + early deal redemption = higher trial-to-paid conversion.

Our bet

If we guide new users through key app features (personalisation, saving restaurants, redeeming deals, leaving reviews) with a structured quest system, while nudging them to redeem at least 2 deals in their first sessions, they'll perceive more value in the app and be significantly more likely to convert from free trial to paid membership.

Competitor analysis showed platforms like Duolingo and Airbnb use guided onboarding flows to keep new users engaged through critical early moments. We had cohort data proving that users who redeemed 2+ deals in trial period converted to paid at rates 4× higher than 1-deal users. The insight was that most users never found that second deal because they didn't explore the full range of features. A quest system would deliberately guide them through the app's core value loop, making trial-to-paid conversion a natural outcome of feature discovery, not an ask.

03 - Research & Analysis

Competitor analysis + cohort data revealed the same pattern: guided onboarding drives trial-to-paid conversion.

Our UX Researcher conducted a competitive analysis of how platforms like Airbnb, DoorDash, and Uber handle new user introduction. The pattern was consistent: all top-performing apps use guided workflows that gradually introduce features and ask for commitment (reviews, saved preferences, bookings) to lock users into habit loops.

Meanwhile, the Data team ran cohort analysis across 6 months of trial data. Key finding: users who redeemed 2+ deals in their trial period converted to paid at 4× the rate of users who only redeemed 1. The problem wasn't the app - it was discovery. Only 15% of new users naturally found a second deal during their trial.

The UX Researcher and I used these insights to shape the quest structure: each task would guide users toward one of the core features, while the 2-deal nudge would be the critical conversion lever.

04 - Stakeholder Management

Negotiating scope and winning buy-in across competing priorities.

This was a three-person squad design challenge: me (designer), the UX Researcher, and the Data Analyst. We partnered with the PM on scope, Engineering on technical feasibility, and the design system team on tokens. The main tension was always around scope: what's essential to drive trial-to-paid conversion vs. what's nice-to-have at v1.

Product Manager

Pushed for more tasks in the quest to expose more features; worried that 5 tasks wasn't enough to move the trial-to-paid needle

Presented cohort data showing 2 deal redemptions was the conversion threshold. Framed the 5 tasks as designed to hit that threshold (personalisation → save → explore → redeem 2× → review). More tasks would create abandonment. Compromise: designed for clean iteration - more tasks could be added post-launch if needed.

Engineering

Raised concerns about quest state management, backend complexity, and integration with analytics pipeline

Kept state tracking simple: each quest just tracked completion boolean + timestamp. Integrated into existing user event system, no new infrastructure. A/B test approach (rolling out to % of users) was partly their suggestion to de-risk the rollout.

UX Researcher

Conducted competitor analysis and shaped quest task wording based on user mental models

Shared findings on how Airbnb, Uber, DoorDash structure onboarding. Collaborated on task copy to match how new users think about the app, not how we talk about features. Their insights shaped task titles and descriptions.

Design System / Umami

Needed to add new semantic color tokens for quest badges and the levels system (taster, foodie, culinary star, gourmet, legend)

Partnered early to define 10 new tokens (2 per level: badge color + confetti animation color). Kept the design system clean and scalable. This became a precedent for future gamification features.

05 - Design Process

Five tasks designed to guide users through core features and reach the 2-deal conversion threshold.

The core constraint was: which 5 actions would most reliably lead a new user from "interested" to "I see the value"? We mapped the cohort data (which actions predicted retention) against the competitor patterns (which features were taught in onboarding flows) to arrive at a clear sequence.

The quest is optional - users can ignore it entirely. But it lives as a non-dismissable banner on Discover (the map view, not Home) for 30 days, because that's where new users naturally land after core onboarding. Users can actually level up without completing the quest - they just need to redeem 2 deals. The quest is an accelerator, not a gate.

Key Design Decisions

01
Number of tasks: Long internal discussions
We debated 3 tasks vs 5 vs 10. PM pushed for more coverage, Engineering wanted fewer for simplicity. We settled on 5: enough to guide through key features (personalisation, saving, deal redemption, review), achievable within trial window, and easily scalable if needed later.
02
The reward debate: No free month, confetti wins
Initial options included an extra free month subscription (discarded - too costly for company, trains users to expect rewards for basic actions). Final: confetti animation on completion + connection to the levels system (badge on profile, visible status). Lightweight but meaningful.
03
The levels system: Deal redemptions unlock achievement
Thresholds based on cohort analysis: Taster (2 deals), Foodie (5), Culinary Star (15), Gourmet (30), Food Legend (55). Users earn levels by redeeming deals, not just completing the quest. Quest completers naturally hit Taster level. But any user can earn levels without the quest. Future ideas kept backlog: ratings and saved restaurants could unlock levels too, testing later.
04
Quest is optional, but well-placed
Not forced into the critical path. Banner on Discover (map view), not Home, because that's where new users land post-onboarding. Non-dismissable for 30 days (enough time to build habit), then users can hide it. Entry point matters enormously for optional features.

Implementation Plan

  • 1A/B test on small % of users (25%) for 3 weeks before full rollout. Validated trial-to-paid conversion lift with control group. Data team set up tracking from day one.
  • 2Quest state tracking integrated into existing user event pipeline - no new backend infrastructure. Simple booleans: quest_viewed, quest_started, quest_task_[1-5]_completed, quest_fully_completed.
  • 3Design system tokens: 10 new semantic colors added to Umami (NeoTaste design system) - 2 per level (badge + confetti). Designed for clean extensibility if levels expand.
  • 4Timeline: ~2 months from initial brief to shipped feature. Scope negotiations and level system work consumed the bulk; UI/interactions were faster.

07 - Conclusion

User motivation doesn't require expensive incentives. It requires clarity and small wins.

The Quest system converted 22% more trial users to paid members - not through discounts or free months, but through guided discovery. The insight that stuck with me: most users didn't need better features. They needed to understand why the features existed and how they'd benefit. The quest is just scaffolding to help them climb faster.

Lessons Learned

01Reward design: achievement systems beat discounts. The initial instinct was to offer an extra free month for completing the quest. We discarded this - too costly, trains users to expect payoffs for basic actions. The confetti animation + levels badge felt lighter but proved more motivating. Users want to feel progress and status, not transactional rewards.
02Optional features need killer entry points. Because the quest was voluntary, its placement was critical. Banner on Discover (map view) saw 48% engagement; early prototypes placed on Home saw 8%. For optional features, discoverability is half the game.
03Scope negotiations revealed the design discipline. PM wanted 10 tasks, Engineering wanted 3, I advocated for 5. The negotiations forced clarity: which 5 actions actually predict conversion? This constraint-driven approach was tighter than expansionist thinking.
01
Levels beyond deal redemptions
Current levels are tied only to deals redeemed. Research opportunity: do ratings, saved restaurants, or reviews predict retention too? If so, unlock new levels through these actions, making progress less dependent on spending.
02
Seasonal/themed quests
v1 quest is permanent. Opportunity to run limited-time themed quests (e.g. "Taco Tuesday Quest" in Q2) to re-engage lapsed users or add seasonal discovery goals.
03
Social progression & friendly competition
Users see their own level, but not friends' levels. Experiment with shared leaderboards or "beat your friend" challenges to add social motivation and drive referrals.
