NeoTaste is a platform to discover restaurants, bars and cafés in major cities, mainly across Germany, with a growing presence in the UK, Netherlands, and Austria. Users get exclusive deals you can only find through NeoTaste. Partner venues use it to fill quieter tables and attract new customers.

01 - Problem Statement

Many users on trial were redeeming just one deal

NeoTaste acquires users primarily through a 1-month free trial. But cohort analysis showed a clear bottleneck: most new users redeemed just 1 deal in their trial period, then uninstalled or disengaged.

This wasn't a product quality problem. The issue was that new users weren't understanding the app's full value quickly enough. They'd discover one deal, use it, and see no reason to return - missing personalisation, saved restaurants, the map view, reviews, and the full range of what NeoTaste offered.

The business problem was conversion. The user problem was that they weren't discovering enough of what the app had to offer. How do we show new users the features that keep people coming back — before their trial runs out?

I led the feature design in partnership with the UX Researcher, Data team, and PM. We grounded the entire solution in behavioural data, not assumptions.

02 - Hypothesis

How we tackled the problem

Our bet

If we guide new users through the app's most important features — setting food preferences, saving restaurants, redeeming deals, leaving reviews — in a structured quest, they'll understand what NeoTaste is actually for. And they'll be more likely to pay for it.

Our data backed this up: users who redeemed at least 2 deals during their free trial converted to paid members at 4× the rate of those who only redeemed 1. Most users never made it to that second deal — not because the app was bad, but because they never discovered it. The quest would change that.

03 - Research & Analysis

Our data and the best apps in the world all pointed the same way

Our UX Researcher looked at how platforms like Airbnb, DoorDash, and Uber introduce new users to their product. The pattern was consistent: all of them guide new users through their key features early, asking for small commitments along the way — reviews, preferences, bookings — so that using the app becomes second nature.

Meanwhile, the Data team ran cohort analysis across 6 months of trial data. Key finding: users who redeemed 2+ deals in their trial period converted to paid at 4× the rate of users who only redeemed 1. The problem wasn't the app - it was discovery. Only 15% of new users naturally found a second deal during their trial.

The UX Researcher and I used these insights to shape the quest structure: each task would guide users toward one of the core features, while the 2-deal nudge would be the critical conversion lever.

04 - Stakeholder Management

Getting everyone aligned before starting to build

We were a core team of three — me, the UX Researcher, and the Data Analyst — working alongside the PM, Engineering, and the design system team.

Scope was the constant tension. The PM wanted more tasks to expose more of the app; I pushed to keep it at 5, backed by data showing that users who redeemed 2 deals converted at 4× the rate of those who only redeemed 1. More tasks would risk abandonment before users hit that threshold. We designed for iteration — if data post-launch showed appetite for more, we could add them.

Engineering suggested rolling out to 25% of new users first before going wide. That gave us a real control group and the flexibility to adjust before fully committing. It was their idea, and the right call.

05 - Design Process

Five steps to help users discover the app and find their reason to stay

The core constraint was: which 5 actions would most reliably lead a new user from "interested" to "I see the value"? We mapped our retention data against what the best apps teach new users early, and arrived at a clear sequence.

The quest is optional - users can ignore it entirely. But it lives as a non-dismissable banner on Discover (the map view, not Home) for 30 days, because that's where new users naturally land after core onboarding. Users can actually level up without completing the quest - they just need to redeem 2 deals. The quest is an accelerator, not a gate.

Key Design Decisions

01
Why 5 tasks — not 3 or 10
We debated 3 tasks vs 5 vs 10. PM pushed for more coverage, Engineering wanted fewer for simplicity. We settled on 5: enough to guide through key features (personalisation, saving, deal redemption, review), achievable within trial window, and easily scalable if needed later.
02
The reward debate: No free month, confetti wins
Initial options included an extra free month subscription (discarded - too costly for company, trains users to expect rewards for basic actions). Final: confetti animation on completion + connection to the levels system (badge on profile, visible status). Lightweight but meaningful.
03
The levels system: how you earn your badge
Thresholds based on our retention data: Taster (2 deals), Foodie (5), Culinary Star (15), Gourmet (30), Food Legend (55). Users earn levels by redeeming deals, not just completing the quest — so anyone can progress. Quest completers naturally hit Taster. Future ideas in the backlog: ratings and saved restaurants could unlock levels too.
04
Quest is optional, but well-placed
Not forced into the critical path. Banner on Discover (map view), not Home, because that's where new users land post-onboarding. Non-dismissable for 30 days (enough time to build habit), then users can hide it. Entry point matters enormously for optional features.

Implementation Plan

  • 1A/B test on small % of users (25%) for 3 weeks before full rollout. Validated trial-to-paid conversion lift with control group. Data team set up tracking from day one.
  • 2Quest progress tracked through the existing analytics pipeline — no new backend infrastructure needed.
  • 3Design system: 10 new colors added to Umami (NeoTaste's component library) — two per level, one for the badge and one for the confetti animation. Designed to scale if new levels are added later.
  • 4Timeline: ~2 months from initial brief to shipped feature. Scope negotiations and level system work consumed the bulk; UI/interactions were faster.
06 - Metrics & KPIs

Trial-to-paid conversion improved by 22%. Deal redemptions tripled in month 1

The Quest launched to 25% of new users first — tested against a group that saw nothing. After 60 days, the results were clear.

The primary goal was trial-to-paid conversion. Secondary: increase deal redemptions in the trial period (we hypothesized this would naturally follow from better feature discovery). The results validated both.

07 - Conclusion

User motivation doesn't require expensive incentives. It requires clarity and small wins

The Quest system converted 22% more trial users to paid members - not through discounts or free months, but through guided discovery. The insight that stuck with me: most users didn't need better features. They needed to understand why the features existed and how they'd benefit. The quest is just scaffolding to help them climb faster.

Lessons Learned

01Reward design: achievement systems beat discounts. The initial instinct was to offer an extra free month for completing the quest. We discarded this - too costly, trains users to expect payoffs for basic actions. The confetti animation + levels badge felt lighter but proved more motivating. Users want to feel progress and status, not transactional rewards.
02Optional features need killer entry points. Because the quest was voluntary, its placement was critical. Banner on Discover (map view) saw 48% engagement; early prototypes placed on Home saw 8%. For optional features, discoverability is half the game.
03The best constraints came from the negotiations, not the brief. PM wanted 10 tasks, Engineering wanted 3, I advocated for 5. Having to justify every task forced clarity: which 5 actions actually predict whether someone stays? That pressure made the design tighter.
01
Levels beyond deal redemptions
Current levels are tied only to deals redeemed. Research opportunity: do ratings, saved restaurants, or reviews predict retention too? If so, unlock new levels through these actions, making progress less dependent on spending.
02
Seasonal/themed quests
v1 quest is permanent. Opportunity to run limited-time themed quests (e.g. "Taco Tuesday Quest" in Q2) to re-engage lapsed users or add seasonal discovery goals.
03
Social progression & friendly competition
Users see their own level, but not friends' levels. Experiment with shared leaderboards or "beat your friend" challenges to add social motivation and drive referrals.
