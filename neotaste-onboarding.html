01 — Context

More than half of new users were leaving before they ever saw what NeoTaste could do.

NeoTaste is a restaurant discovery and deals platform used by hundreds of thousands of food lovers across Germany. Users sign up to explore local restaurants, unlock exclusive deals, and book tables directly in the app.

When I joined the Growth team in early 2024, the data told a clear story: 54% of newly registered users dropped off during onboarding — never reaching the home feed and therefore never experiencing the product's core value.

The existing flow had grown organically over 3 years. What started as a simple 3-step setup had ballooned into a 7-screen sequence with permission requests, lengthy form fields, and marketing opt-ins — all before showing the user a single restaurant.

I led this project as the sole product designer on the Growth team, working closely with the Growth PM and a data analyst, and aligning with engineering early to define what was feasible to ship within the quarter.

Old onboarding — 7 steps
02 — Hypothesis

Value first, permissions later.

Our bet

Simplifying the flow and showing value before asking for permissions or data would significantly reduce drop-off and increase Day-1 activation.

Session recordings showed most abandonment occurred when permission screens appeared before users understood what the app did. Benchmark research from similar restaurant discovery apps showed value-first onboarding flows achieved 4x higher completion rates. We believed that letting users see real restaurants and deals in the first 15 seconds would establish trust, making them more willing to grant permissions and complete signup.

03 — Research & Analysis

Session recordings and eight user interviews surfaced the same friction points again and again.

Before touching a wireframe, I spent two weeks in discovery. I reviewed session recordings in Hotjar, analysed the step-by-step funnel in Mixpanel, and ran eight 45-minute moderated interviews with recently churned users recruited through a post-signup survey.

The funnel data confirmed that step 3 (location permissions) and step 5 (notification opt-in) had the highest abandonment. The interviews told us why: users felt the app was asking for too much before proving its worth.

I presented the findings to the Growth PM and engineering lead before touching a wireframe — getting alignment on the root cause early meant the solution brief was agreed before I opened Figma.

"I didn't understand why it needed my location before I'd even seen what the app looked like. I just closed it."

04 — Stakeholder Management

Aligning across product, engineering, and marketing on scope and tradeoffs.

This redesign touched multiple teams with competing priorities. Early alignment on the research findings was critical — I shared session recordings and funnel data with stakeholders before proposing solutions, which helped frame the conversation around user behavior rather than opinions.

Product Manager

Wanted to keep location permission early (needed to optimize local restaurant recommendations)

Compromised: location request was deferred to first search, where context makes the ask feel natural. Engineering and I prototyped this to prove the pattern worked on iOS guidelines.

Engineering Lead

Flagged concern about animation complexity in the splash screen and feared scope creep

Worked iteratively on prototypes showing animation performance on low-end devices; scoped progressive profiling to v2 to keep sprint tight.

Growth/Marketing

Wanted more brand touchpoints and email capture in the flow

Added a subtle brand moment in the splash screen and scheduled email capture for Day 3 push via post-signup behavioral trigger, rather than in-flow.

Data Team

Needed new event tracking to measure progressive profiling behavior

Iterated on analytics spec in weekly syncs; launched with core metrics first, refined tracking in subsequent sprints.

05 — Design Process

From 7 friction-heavy steps to 3 — with value shown before anything is asked.

The guiding principle became: earn the right to ask. Show the product first. Let users experience the core value — browsing nearby restaurants and deals — before requesting permissions or preferences.

I mapped three concept directions, workshopped them with the PM and engineering lead, and moved into prototype testing within two weeks. Each concept was tested with 6 participants in unmoderated sessions via Maze.

Design Solution

The redesigned onboarding opens with an animated restaurant card carousel — real venues from the user's region, showing deal prices and cuisine types. The first 15 seconds are spent watching the product work, not filling out a form.

Account creation was stripped to the minimum: email and password, plus Apple/Google sign-in. Dietary preferences were removed entirely from onboarding and replaced by a progressive profiling system that learns from saved items and search behaviour over the first week.

Permission asks were deferred to context: location is requested the first time a user searches, push notifications are requested the first time a user saves a deal. Both convert at over 60% in this contextual state vs. under 30% in the old flow.

Implementation Plan

  • 1Phased rollout: 20% traffic A/B test for 2 weeks to validate metrics and catch edge cases in low-traffic scenarios. Required close coordination with Data and QA teams to instrument event tracking for all permission flows.
  • 2Engineering flagged animation performance risk on older Android devices. Mitigated by optimizing carousel re-renders and using CSS transforms instead of JavaScript animations. Tested on devices with 2GB RAM.
  • 3Scoped progressive profiling to v2 after engineering estimated 3+ weeks for full implementation. Launched with basic behavior-based preferences collection instead of explicit form.
  • 4Backend permission system update required changes to the location services API. Coordinated timing so dependent features (search filtering) shipped in same release.

07 — Conclusion

Simplifying early experiences unlocks disproportionate value.

This redesign proved that onboarding drop-off wasn't about users not understanding NeoTaste — it was about friction points and broken trust. By showing value first and asking for permissions in context, we reduced abandonment by 63% and nearly doubled Day-1 activation. Beyond the metrics, the 4.6-star App Store rating reflected a better first impression.

Lessons Learned

01Data + clips > opinions. Session recordings made the case for cutting the dietary preferences screen far more compelling than a slide deck. When stakeholders saw three users abandon at the same screen, alignment happened in minutes.
02Context changes everything for permissions. The same location permission that converted at 28% on the splash screen converted at 61% when requested during search. Timing and context, not design, drive permission grant rates.
03Scope is a design decision. Progressive profiling was the tightest design problem (learning preferences without forms). Descoping it to v2 kept the sprint realistic and the core flow laser-focused. The perfect is the enemy of the shipped.
01
Progressive profiling
Collect dietary preferences and cuisine interests through in-app behaviour — saves, searches, bookings — rather than a form during onboarding. Descoped from v1, this would close the data gap left by removing the preferences screen.
02
Paywall trigger A/B test
The free trial currently appears mid-onboarding. Testing whether deferring it until after a user saves their first deal improves both conversion rate and retention quality is the natural next test.
03
Location pre-permission screen
A designed screen explaining why location improves the experience — rather than hitting users with the raw iOS system prompt. Benchmarks suggest this could improve grant rates by 15–20%.
