Finding the right incentive to double referral volume

NeoTaste is a platform to discover restaurants, bars and cafés in major cities — mainly across Germany, with a growing presence in the UK, Netherlands, and Austria. Users get exclusive deals you can only find through NeoTaste. Partner venues use it to fill quieter tables and attract new customers.

01 - Context

NeoTaste's referral program was working - just not well enough.

With 2 million users on the platform, word-of-mouth was already one of NeoTaste's strongest acquisition channels. But the existing referral program - which rewarded both the referrer and the new user with one free month of subscription - had a participation rate of just 2.1%.

The Growth team hypothesised that the subscription reward, while valuable, was too abstract. A user who wasn't already convinced of NeoTaste's ongoing value might not be moved by the promise of more time in an app they're still evaluating. A cash reward - €10 transferred directly to the user's account - might drive significantly higher participation.

I designed both variants and helped set up the test alongside the Growth Engineer and Data Analyst.

02 - Hypothesis

Immediate cash incentives would drive more referrals than delayed value.

Our bet

Offering €10 cash per referral (credited immediately) would outperform a 1-month free subscription in referral conversion rates.

The existing referral program was only getting 2.1% of active users to actually share. Research backs up the intuition: people respond more to a reward they can use right now than a promise of future value. Cash is also simpler to understand — there's no "how do I claim this?" moment. The free month has steps; €10 doesn't.

Test Design

We designed the test around a single primary metric: referral conversion rate - the percentage of active users who shared their referral link and had at least one friend sign up. Secondary metrics tracked new user quality: D7 retention and first-month engagement of referred users.

03 - Stakeholder Management

Coordinating across Growth, Legal, and Product before a single pixel was touched.

This test required alignment across teams with very different priorities. Growth wanted to move fast; Legal had compliance concerns about cash incentives; Engineering needed to scope the payment infrastructure; and Product wanted to protect the brand. One thing we had to acknowledge upfront: claiming cash was instant, while claiming the free month had extra steps — that difference in experience was itself a variable we couldn't fully control.

Growth Team (Referral Program Owner)

Wanted to move fast with test launch; believed cash would be unambiguous winner

Coordinated weekly test planning syncs with all teams upfront. Legal review timeline was identified as critical path and escalated early to compress the 2-week delay to 10 days. Clear success criteria documented before launch.

Legal

Needed to sign off on cash incentive variant; required compliance review

Prepared detailed test spec and use cases upfront so Legal could review async. Positioned test as time-boxed and reversible if issues arose. Legal approved in 10 days with contingency clauses documented.

Engineering

Flagged complexity of real-time payment crediting system; estimated 2-week implementation

Scoped a simplified v1: cash credited asynchronously to user account on next login, rather than real-time. Reduced implementation to 5 days. Documented as limitation in test results readout.

Product & Marketing

Concerned about brand positioning; wanted incentive framed around discovery and community, not commoditized cash

Agreed on messaging: €10 positioned as "money to explore" (toward deals/meals) rather than generic "cashback." Test positioned as exploring incentive mechanics, not locking brand strategy long-term.

04 - A/B Test Results

€10 drove twice as many referrals. But the free month brought better users.

The results after 30 days were clear on the primary metric - and nuanced on the secondary ones. Variant B (€10 cash) dramatically outperformed on conversion rate. But the referred users it attracted showed meaningfully lower retention and engagement at Day 30.

The PM, Data Analyst, and I reviewed the findings together - the tension between volume and user quality wasn't immediately obvious in the raw numbers, and needed careful framing before we took it to the leadership team.

05 - Design Process

From A/B results to a hybrid recommendation.

The test revealed a clear trade-off: the €10 cash variant drove a 5.2% referral rate but attracted users who weren't sticking around long-term. The 1-month free subscription variant saw only 2.3% referral rate, but 52% of those referred users were still active after 7 days — vs. 38% for the cash group.

Rather than choosing the "winner," we designed a combined incentive: €10 cash credit for the referrer (addressing the activation barrier) plus one free month for the new user (ensuring product exposure and quality). This addressed both sides of the trade-off.

The combined approach was prototyped in the existing referral UI framework, tested with 15 users in moderated sessions, and launched to 100% in Q4 2024.

Implementation & Rollout

  • 1Updated referral flow copy and reward display to show both incentives (€10 for referrer + free month for new user). Minimal code changes leveraged existing variant infrastructure from the A/B test.
  • 2Coordinated payment crediting: €10 credited to referrer account on referred friend's first login (asynchronous, as engineered for test). Subscription added automatically to new user account on signup.
  • 3Launched with clear success tracking: referral rate as primary metric, with secondary tracking on cost per acquired user and 7-day retention of referred users. The combined approach drove a 148% increase in total referrals compared to the original program.

07 - Conclusion

Sometimes the right answer is: test both, then take the best of each.

This project showed that better solutions exist when you measure both sides of a trade-off. Cash drove volume but brought users who didn't stick around. The free month preserved quality but didn't move enough people to act. By testing both and reading the data carefully, we designed a combined approach that hit 5.2% referral rate (2.5× the original baseline) while keeping 46% of referred users still active after 7 days.

Lessons Learned

01Test both sides of the trade-off. If we'd stopped at the first test, we'd have shipped the cash incentive and missed the quality signal. By designing a second variant that prioritized quality, we learned there was room for a blended solution.
02Isolate the variable you're testing. The different UX paths for redemption (instant cash vs. subscription confirmation) introduced uncontrolled friction. For future tests, we'd standardize UX and test only the incentive mechanic to get cleaner signals.
03Stakeholder alignment upfront saves weeks. Legal review of the cash incentive was our critical path. By getting all stakeholders in a room at the start and documenting the timeline, we compressed a 2-week delay to 10 days and kept the test schedule intact.
01
Market-level segmentation
The incentive that works in Berlin may not land the same way in Hamburg or Munich. A follow-up test with city-level segmentation was already scoped at the time of writing.
02
Tiered referral system
Rewarding users who consistently refer high-retention friends differently from one-and-done referrers. Requires enriching the referral data model with downstream retention signals for referred users.
03
High-intent surface expansion
Currently triggered from settings and post-deal. Testing in-context placements - after a 5-star experience or completing a streak - as higher-intent sharing moments.
