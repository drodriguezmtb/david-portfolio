01 — Context

Users were leaving after three months. Not because the product was bad — but because it stopped challenging them.

Activity data showed that nearly 50% of users dropped off within the first three months. Only 10% were still active after 22 months. Program completion rates were low — suggesting users either found the content too long, or lacked the motivation to keep going.

A deeper look revealed a second problem: 62% of Gymondo's user base were beginners, and most new features were built for them. The 33% of intermediate users felt underserved — and were quietly leaving for competitors.

I led the project end-to-end — from data analysis and research through to launch — working with the PM, a data analyst, and iOS and Android engineers. The retention case and proposed solution were presented to the leadership team before the project was greenlit.

02 — Research

We asked users directly. The answer was clear: they wanted challenges.

We ran a survey asking users which potential features — ones Gymondo didn't yet have — they'd find most valuable. The top answer for both beginners and intermediate users was: "Challenges to build lifestyle habits."

Most competitors already had some form of challenges. We classified them into three types: create-your-own, activity-based, and curated workout challenges.

For the MVP, we scoped down to the two most implementable types — curated workouts and activity-based — deferring custom challenges to a later phase. This scoping call was made jointly with the PM to balance speed to market against feature completeness.

03 — Challenges

Designing for two user groups with incompatible motivations.

The core tension was between competitive athletes who wanted leaderboards and personal-best tracking, and casual users who wanted encouragement without pressure. Early concepts that tried to serve both groups ended up working for neither. After two rounds of testing, we made the call to launch with individual progress only and defer the social layer to v2.

A second challenge emerged mid-project: the backend team flagged that recurring challenge logic was substantially more complex than estimated, requiring us to redesign the trigger system two weeks before dev handoff. We simplified to fixed-duration challenges and documented the dynamic version as a v2 spec.

04 — Solution

Designing for two types of motivation — and shipping the one that tested best.

The user flow handled two challenge types with distinct mechanics: curated workouts (a structured sequence over days/weeks) and activity-based challenges (tied to tracked movement data). We explored how users would discover, join, track, and complete each — and how challenges could coexist with Programs without cannibalising it.

We moved quickly from rough concepts to internal review, designing five key states: Home (available), Home (active), Not joined, Joined + activity, and Completed.

Based on testing, we launched with Curated Challenges only. The activity-based type needed further design work to resolve a guidance problem. We defined all card states and progress components, and created a new set of backgrounds and achievement badges in collaboration with the graphic design team.

05 — Testing

Five users. Four scenarios. Two very different results.

We ran prototype testing with 5 users across 4 scenarios: initial discovery, partial completion, full completion, and the alternative challenge type.

✅
Curated Challenge understood
Users had all the info they needed to join. Its similarity to the Programs concept made it feel immediately familiar.
❌
Activity Challenge confused users
The graph was shown without enough guidance. Users felt lost — they saw data but didn't know what action to take to fill it.
🔀
Workout order not important
Some users followed the given order top-to-bottom; others picked workouts based on difficulty. Vertical navigation felt limiting.
🎉
Completion needs more celebration
The completed state felt flat. Users expected a stronger reward moment — a signal that they'd achieved something meaningful.

"Curated was immediately understood. Activity felt like a dead end — users saw the graph but had no idea what to do next."

06 — Results

Retention improved. A side effect emerged. Both informed what came next.

Challenges shipped and performed well on the main metric — but surfaced an interesting tension with Programs that needed attention. The findings directly informed the next roadmap priority: resolving the cannibalisation effect became the following design sprint.

07 — Next Steps

The v1 infrastructure was built with v2 in mind from day one.

01
Social challenges
Community leaderboards and friend-vs-friend challenges. The individual-progress infrastructure was built to support this without significant rearchitecting — only the social layer and notification system need adding.
02
Challenge-driven personalisation
Users who consistently complete endurance challenges could be automatically routed toward more advanced training tracks — closing the loop between the engagement layer and the core product value proposition.
03
Activity-based challenge type
Deprioritised after testing revealed a guidance problem. With the right onboarding overlay and progress visualisation resolved, this is the next challenge type ready to ship and the highest-voted feature in post-launch user surveys.
