01 — Context

Users were leaving after three months. Not because the product was bad — but because it stopped challenging them.

Activity data showed that nearly 50% of users dropped off within the first three months. Only 10% were still active after 22 months. Program completion rates were low — suggesting users either found the content too long, or lacked the motivation to keep going.

A deeper look revealed a second problem: 62% of Gymondo's user base were beginners, and most new features were built for them. The 33% of intermediate users felt underserved — and were quietly leaving for competitors.

02 — Research

We asked users directly. The answer was clear: they wanted challenges.

We ran a survey asking users which potential features — ones Gymondo didn't yet have — they'd find most valuable. The top answer for both beginners and intermediate users was: "Challenges to build lifestyle habits."

Most competitors already had some form of challenges. We classified them into three types: create-your-own, activity-based, and curated workout challenges.

For the MVP, we focused on the two most implementable types — curated workouts and activity-based — deferring custom challenges to a later phase.

Survey results + competitor benchmark
03 — UX Design

Designing for two types of motivation — and knowing which to prioritise.

The user flow needed to handle two challenge types with distinct mechanics: curated workouts (a structured sequence over days/weeks) and activity-based challenges (tied to tracked movement data).

We explored how users would discover, join, track, and complete each type — and how challenges could coexist with the existing Programs concept without cannibalising it.

User flow diagram — both challenge types
04 — Wireframing

Fast concepts. Internal feedback. Then a real prototype.

We moved quickly from concept to internal review. The first wireframes were intentionally rough — the goal was to get something testable with colleagues before investing in high-fidelity work.

We designed for five key states: Home (available), Home (active), Not joined, Joined + activity, and Completed.

After a first UI iteration and internal feedback pass, we prepared a prototype for user testing with the introduction of both challenge types.

Wireframe exploration — challenge states
05 — Testing

Five users. Four scenarios. Two very different results.

We ran prototype testing with 5 users across 4 scenarios: initial discovery, partial completion, full completion, and the alternative challenge type.

✅
Curated Challenge understood
Users had all the info they needed to join. Its similarity to the Programs concept made it feel immediately familiar.
❌
Activity Challenge confused users
The graph was shown without enough guidance. Users felt lost — they saw data but didn't know what action to take to fill it.
🔀
Workout order not important
Some users followed the given order top-to-bottom; others picked workouts based on difficulty. Vertical navigation felt limiting.
🎉
Completion needs more celebration
The completed state felt flat. Users expected a stronger reward moment — a signal that they'd achieved something meaningful.

"Curated was immediately understood. Activity felt like a dead end — users saw the graph but had no idea what to do next."

06 — Final Design

One challenge type launched. Built modularly for what comes next.

Based on testing, we launched with Curated Challenges only. The activity-based type needed further design work to resolve the guidance problem before being production-ready.

We defined all states for the new Challenges cards and the progress card, and created a new set of backgrounds and achievement badges in collaboration with the graphic design team.

Working closely with devs was especially important here — we ran collaborative sessions to resolve exactly how the header and navigation should behave on the Challenge detail screen during scroll.

Final challenge screens — all states
07 — Results

Retention improved. A side effect emerged. Both informed what came next.

Challenges shipped and performed well on the main metric — but surfaced an interesting tension with Programs that needed attention.
